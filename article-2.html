Track Everything: Engineering Your Physique

From February of 2015 until July of 2016, I tracked my caloric intake every day on MyFitnessPal. Throughout the 475 days, there were ups and downs, but at the end I had learned so much that it was invaluably worth the work. I went through phases where I was eating far too little. During my junior year of high school cross country season, I dropped to 156 lbs at my lowest. I'm 6' 1", so this was basically the lightest I could have been before I started pushing the boundaries of being too lean for what is natural and healthy. I also went through periods of spinning my wheels, cutting heavily for a few days only to overeat in the next few. Throughout this all, there was a net benefit. I started at around 190 lbs with no muscle, went down to 156 lbs (still no muscle - imagine a cross country stereotype), and ended my tracking endeavor at 170 lbs with good leanness. I got a PR of 5:52 in the mile. I went to hell and back. Here's how you can do it more easily.

 

Track everything, but don't obsess. Remember that at the end of the day, if you aren't overweight and are just trying to achieve something great, then you really have nothing to worry about. If you are healthy, realize that people care about fitness a lot less than you think. It doesn't really matter. The only meaning that fitness haves is that which you give to it. Which is part of why it does have meaning. It's a paradox because fitness is valuable because it is optional, so you have to be willing to put in the work to achieve something great without anyone making you do it.

 

The most important benefit of tracking my calorie intake is that it keeps me consistent. It turns eating nutritiously from a thing that you some days to a lifestyle that you do almost every day. Nobody is perfect; I have been known to eat entire pizzas and pints of Ben and Jerry's. But if those cases of cheating on your diet are kept to once a week or less, and you aren't going wayyyyy overboard (5000+ calories), then you won't really do any damage because the majority of your days will be focused. I also track all of my lifting workouts. Gaining strength is very important to building muscle. So by tracking the amount which you lift in each workout you can increase the intensity at a steady rate. You will also be more consistent and less likely to miss workouts because you're holding yourself accountable to a schedule. Overall, tracking both caloric intake and the weight you lift will keep you consistent and determined, allowing you to see your progress over time.
