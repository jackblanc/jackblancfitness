Intermittent Fasting: The most powerful tool for your diet

What if I told you that if you skipped breakfast and ate your first meal at around 2:00 PM every day, you would live forever, have a higher IQ, and be able to lift cars? You would probably be very quick to call BS. After all, isn't breakfast the most important meal of the day? Well although I did make up those benefits, skipping breakfast, known officially as intermittent fasting, can actually have some profound health benefits. It is one of the easiest ways to reduce the stress of eating healthy, especially if you are attempting to lose weight. In this article I will discuss the benefits and address some concerns.

 

First, how to go about intermittent fasting in the first place. Most people agree that the 16/8 fast is the best way to go. This means fasting sixteen hours a day and eating during eight. What does this look like? Finish your last meal by 8:00 PM and eat your first meal at 12:00PM the next day. It isn't really that difficult to do. Wake up in the morning, drink black coffee to suppress your appetite, drink a lot of water throughout the day, and next thing you know it'll be time for lunch. Or a very late breakfast. Call it what you want. Now, the benefits and concerns addressed.

 

Benefit #1: It makes everything much more simple. Putting aside any concerns you may have, consider this logic. Say you are trying to cut body fat, therefore eating at a caloric deficit that puts your daily intake goal at 2000 calories. If you were to eat six meals a day, each would be just over 300 calories. That doesn't sound very filling to me. On the other hand, say you eat two 900 calorie meals and a 200 calorie snack. There is a lot more flexibility here to eat the foods you actually enjoy, and eating more at once will make you actually feel satisfied with the meal. Sounds pretty great, right?

 

Benefit #2: The fasted state will allow you to burn body fat. Now I'm not claiming that the pounds will fly off, but everything helps, right? Fasting changes your hormones. It lowers insulin, increases growth hormones, and increases norepinephrine (noradrenaline) which all increase the utilization of fat stores in the body as energy. Basically, as well as making it easier to hit a caloric deficit, fasting can increase your metabolic rate by 3.6% to 14%.

 

Benefit #3: Intermittent fasting is good for your brain. It increases metabolic features which are known to be good for brain health. Reduced oxidative stress, reduced inflammation, reduced blood sugar and reduced insulin resistance all help the brain function better. Studies in rats have shown intermittent fasting to increase the growth of new nerve cells. In my opinion, intermittent fasting also has the psychological benefit of helping you focus. Have you ever been so lost in your work that by the time you check the clock, hours have flown by? Intermittent fasting can help you reach that state of "flow" because in the fasted state, you are more likely to forget about the outside world. You don't have to worry about food at all. Additionally, have you ever had a meal and felt "food coma" after? You probably weren't very motivated to do anything productive in that state. But in the fasted state I have found myself to be sharper.

 

Common Concern #1: How the hell am I going to make it through the day without eating? A valid concern indeed. However, I dare you to try it yourself for at least three days, maybe five, to see if you get used to it. Because your body and mind are conditioned to expect food in a certain pattern, breaking that pattern will be initially difficult. If you've eaten breakfast every day of your life, you will have to break that habit. But after a few days, I'd bet that things start to normalize. In addition, drinking black coffee and a lot of water can help to suppress your appetite throughout the fasted state.

 

Common Concern #2: But wait - I thought that if I went more than an hour without 20g of protein, I'll shrivel up into nothing and lose all my gains? Maybe your concern isn't that exaggerated, but many people worry that intermittent fasting will cause muscle loss. In fact, the opposite is true. This concern originates from the idea that you need a constant stream of amino acids to build and maintain muscle mass. However, protein is broken down very slowly in the body. In your "feeding" window if you eat say 160 grams of protein, your body will be breaking down that fuel for between 16 and 24 hours. Some studies have even shown increases in muscle mass and decreases in fat stores that are solely the result of fasting.

 

Common Concern #3: Eating frequently "stokes the metabolic fire," causing you to burn more calories throughout the day. The idea originates from the fact that eating causes you to burn calories as you break down the food. This is known as the Thermic Effect of Food. However, studies have shown that the TEF will always be the same. If you eat two 900 calorie meals, you'd have a larger, longer lasting TEF boost, but only a few times a day. If you eat six 300 calorie meals, you would have smaller TEF boosts throughout the entire day. In both cases, the total effect is equal.
